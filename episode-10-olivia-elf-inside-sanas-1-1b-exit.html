Inside Sana's $1.1B Exit
The Operating Principles That Made It

Olivia Elf joined Sana when it was 20 people and stayed five years, through Series A all the way to a $1.1 billion acquisition by Workday. The operating principles that made it: giving away your Legos, Sanyans have no toes, fully uncapped roles, and why everything has to be fractionally beautiful.

Show Notes

About This Episode

Olivia Elf joined Sana in 2021 when it was 20 people. She came in as Chief of Staff, moved through Director of Operations to VP Operations, and stayed five years, through Series A all the way to a $1.1 billion acquisition by Workday, one of the largest AI exits Europe has produced. In this episode she joins Ceci and Bea to walk through what that arc actually looked like from the operating seat.

This is a conversation about principles more than tactics. Sana ran on a small set of deliberately extreme operating ideas, and Olivia spent five years living inside them. Cecilia (FD at PortalOne, ex-VC at Talis Capital) and Bea (Chief of Staff at Praktika, ex-VC at Lakestar) press on each one: where it comes from, how you instil it in people, and where it would break. They also get the honest version of the acquisition, including the hardest day of Olivia's career.

What We Cover in This Episode

Five Years, Four Roles, One Company

Olivia is the rare operator who kept moving up inside one company instead of job-hopping. She knew early that if Sana survived, she would still be there, and she is open that there was no grand plan beyond a commitment to see it through. Her role model is Hillary McAdams, once on Sana's board, who spent the first 18 years of her career at Oracle when it was still small. That kind of long-arc loyalty is what Olivia says she is "obsessed with", and it shaped how she thought about staying.

Loyalty, Agency and the People Who Stay

Ceci goes off-script to ask why loyalty seems to be fading between generations. Olivia's answer: hype cycles are shorter now, and the younger generation tends to chase the next greener thing instead of trusting they have the agency to build something where they already are. She has run thousands of interviews at Sana, and the trait she screens hardest for is people who stayed when things were tough or boring and still felt they could change things. People who jump ship rather than trust that agency, she says, are harder and less fun to work with.

Why She Joined Sana: Joel's Pitch and "No Compromises"

Two things pulled her in. First, a genuine 2021 conviction that AI would matter more than anything else. Second, and stronger, her first coffee with founder and CEO Joel, who told her they would build a company on the principles they actually believed in: a place where people could do their life's work, with no compromises and extreme principles. She could not think about anything else for weeks, dropped what she was doing, and took the bet. Everyone told them they would have to give those principles up eventually. They never did, which Olivia describes as very hard, and fun.

"If You're Doing Today What You Did Six Months Ago, You're Failing"

Olivia said this to the hosts before recording, and it turns out to be the operating logic underneath everything else. The point is not constant churn for its own sake. It is that ownership is temporary by design, so if your work has not changed in six months, you have stopped handing things off and stopped finding harder problems.

Giving Away Your Legos

The mechanic that makes the six-month rule work. You pick something up, improve it, make it yours, package it, then automate it, outsource it or hand it to someone else, and go find the next hard thing. That is how Sana's DRI (directly responsible individual) areas work. The side effects are the interesting part: nobody is territorial because there is no turf to protect, everything gets quietly rebuilt when it changes hands so nothing calcifies into "we do it this way because we always have", and nobody is scared to pick up hard problems because they know they will not own them forever. It works, Olivia says, because Sana has always been growing, and a growing company is far easier to run than a stagnant one.

"Sanyans Have No Toes"

The principle that keeps the org non-territorial. Because Sanyans have no toes, anyone can jump on any problem they see, and no one can say "that's mine, stay off it". Paired with undefined, uncapped roles (nobody is "head of finance" or "senior manager of whatever", you just are), it means every person treats every problem as theirs to solve. Olivia is clear this does not magically erase ego, it means the company is deliberate about not building the kind of org where turf exists.

Fully Uncapped: Context, Not Control

Sana hires generalists even for specialised roles, which Olivia calls one of her strongest operating principles. Tightly defined roles create a ceiling; generalists let the company change strategy without being stuck with the wrong people. The second half is how you run them: give context, not control. You hand a team the north star, the milestone, the problem as you understand it, and let them run. It produces better results because it gives full ownership, and it produces genuinely uncapped people because they have to range across domains and talk to everyone to solve it. Ceci pressure-tests the logic: if a function is full, how is anyone uncapped? The answer is that generalists move sideways into whatever problem is open, so the ceiling is the company's, not the role's.

Find a Leader Who Believes in You More Than You Do

Olivia's single biggest piece of career advice. The most important thing you can do joining a company is find a leader who believes in you more than you believe in yourself. They push you into things you would have hesitated to take, and you borrow their certainty until you build your own. Sana trains its leaders to do this. Her practical test for anyone joining a company: know who your leader will be and what their traits are, and diligence that above almost everything else.

Everything Must Be Fractionally Beautiful

Olivia's favourite saying is "how you spend your days is how you spend your life", which is why she sees building a company as one of the most meaningful things you can do. Beauty and joy are ends in themselves at Sana, not a means to something else. Concretely: a Sana stamp under every ceramic, mission patches for each team, every ritual named and beautiful if you zoom in. Take the whole company, zoom into the smallest component, and that component has to be beautiful too. When Ceci plays devil's advocate as the finance person on cost and focus, Olivia argues there is no tension: a neatly designed process that scales and removes friction is one of the most beautiful things there is, and in ops the best work is often invisible. Her proudest example is the "promo crash", where senior leaders ambush a soon-to-be-promoted person on what they think is a routine one-on-one.

Inside the $1.1B Workday Acquisition

From the ops seat, the acquisition came in eras. A roughly two-month due diligence era that was hectic and intense. Then a strange "limbo" era after DD closed but before the deal was announced to the world and to the rest of Sana, when they could not run business as usual and Olivia, who works on radical transparency and "cannot keep two stories in her head", had to sit on the biggest news in the company. Since then it has been about integrating Sana into Workday while still operating fairly independently under Joel, who has taken on the Chief AI Officer role at Workday. Olivia calls it an operator's dream and a crash course in scale: you spend years imagining what 2,000 or 4,000 people feels like, then suddenly you are part of 22,000.

The Hardest Day: Announcing the Deal

The clearest before-and-after was the day they could finally tell the rest of the Sanians. Olivia calls it one of the hardest days of her career, because they knew everyone would react strongly and that the reactions would be genuinely mixed: pride, joy and celebration alongside worry and mourning. Holding that, knowing it was coming and not being able to soften it, was the part that stuck with her.

Why She Stayed Instead of Cashing Out

A lot of people exit and retire. Olivia does not feel ready, and cannot think of anything more fun than integrating a company she knows every corner of into a whole new world. She frames it the way she frames everything, as the next level of the game: when she stands between two paths, she picks the one that makes the better story. Right now that means seeing the integration through and trying to make the Sana acquisition the most successful of all time.

Building Before the AI Wave, and From Europe

Sana was founded before AI hit consumer consciousness, and Olivia says the foundation was intentional from day one. When she joined at 20 people and did a one-on-one with everyone, she got as many different answers about what they were building as there were people, but identical answers about why they were there. They had a company before they had a product, built for chaos, pivots and no product-market fit, so it would thrive in the harshest environments. On Europe versus the US, she pushes back on the psychopath-founder myth: smaller markets force global thinking early, and she would never build anywhere but Sweden. Ceci adds that the Nordic approach brings more elegance, less 150-hour-week grit, and that values-led companies tend to build something better.

The Montessori Theory of Operators

Olivia was a Montessori kid, schooled in a way where nobody tells you the problem, the solution or the format, you find all of it yourself. She thinks that is exactly the muscle running a company demands, because the hardest part is defining the problem and prioritising, not solving it. Ceci, who went through a traditional Italian education that praises you for following the path, says she had to actively un-learn it to think creatively, and only the rebel version of herself found her own way. They agree Montessori is better suited to an AI era that rewards stimulating creativity over teaching fixed skills.

Rapid Fire

Operator craft when the ground keeps moving: lean into chaos rather than control it, and hire people who thrive in it, not just tolerate it. Favourite AI tool for the day to day, with Sana not allowed as an answer: the Claude chat window. And the one thing every Chief of Staff should stop doing in 2026: Olivia refuses to answer, on the grounds that it is like asking how long a rope is.

I always say to the ops team at Sana, we've done the best work if nobody notices us.

Olivia [22:53]

Inside the $1.1B Workday Acquisition

Olivia [24:53]

There are a bunch of eras in this acquisition. First, there was the due diligence era, which lasted about two months. That was a very hectic, very intense era. I'm a person who normally works with a lot of transparency, it sounds like I'm patting myself on the shoulder, but I cannot keep two stories in my head, so I normally just say whatever's on my mind. The Hardest Day: Announcing the Deal

Olivia [25:49]

We knew the reactions were going to be very strong, so that was a tough one. After that there hasn't been a very clear before-and-after moment or week. Ever since, we've just worked on integrating Sana into Workday. As an operator you're always thinking, oh, when we IPO, when we're 2,000 people, when we're 4,000, it's just scale, scale, scale. And then all of a sudden it's not incremental anymore. It's not, oh, we grew by 10 people last week, it's, all of a sudden we're 22,000 people. It's a very fun and crazy period to get to go through.

Ceci [27:44]

Olivia [28:22]

Building Before the AI Wave, From Europe

Olivia [32:08]
